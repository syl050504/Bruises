Nan Goldin — Nan One Month After Being Battered, 1984 Heart-shaped bruise, thigh

A bruise is a record. The body keeps evidence of contact — of force, of accident, of care gone wrong. Unlike a cut, it doesn't break the surface. It stays under the skin, pooling color through yellow, purple, green, brown, before it disappears.

Nan Goldin understood this. In 1984, one month after being beaten by her boyfriend, she photographed herself: red lipstick, pearl necklace, two black eyes. The image became one of the most important photographs of the twentieth century — not because it is shocking, but because it refuses to look away. It insists on being seen.

This archive collects bruises without hierarchy. Sport, accident, medical procedure, unknown cause. The body as site of ordinary life. What happened here? How many days ago? The colors tell time.
